Then this is actually moving in the right direction. For a reports screen, the calmer/static feel is appropriate.

The issue is not that it’s bad — it’s that it currently looks like:

* raw KPI tiles
* without storytelling
* without visual hierarchy
* without insight generation

A reporting page should answer:

* How are we doing?
* What changed?
* Where are we losing money?
* What should leadership pay attention to?

Right now it only answers:

* “Here are some numbers.”

For reports specifically, I would recommend:

Top Section — Executive Snapshot
Keep:

* Claims Submitted
* Claims Paid
* Denials/Rejections
* Charges Submitted
* Payments Posted

But improve them with:

* trends
* comparison to prior month
* color indicators
* mini graphs/sparklines

Example:

* Claims Paid
  $84,220
  ↑ 14% from April

That immediately makes the dashboard feel intelligent.

Then add:

* Outstanding AR
* Collection Rate
* Average Days in AR
* Net Collection %
* Top Denial Reason

Those are the metrics owners actually care about.

Second Section — Operational Health
This is where:

* payer calls
* follow-up activity
* unresolved claims
* ERA lag
* auth issues

belong.

Smaller cards.
Less emphasis.

Third Section — Trends & Visuals
This page is missing charts entirely.

You need:

* charges vs payments over time
* denials trend
* payer mix
* top CARCs
* aging buckets
* clinician productivity
* collections by payer

Without charts, reports feel unfinished.

Also:
The spacing is too large vertically.

You could reduce dashboard height by 30–40%.

Right now:
each card consumes too much dead space.

Another thing:
You should make reports role-aware.

Practice Owner sees:

* revenue
* profitability
* payer performance
* clinician output
* AR trends

Biller sees:

* denial categories
* aging
* unresolved queues
* follow-up productivity

Clinician sees:

* sessions completed
* unsigned notes
* auth expirations
* collection summaries
* attendance/no-show trends

Most EHRs fail because everyone sees the same reporting surface.

Behavioral health users want:

* simplicity
* relevance
* emotional clarity

not enterprise BI overload.

Another major recommendation:

Use natural language insight banners.

Example:

* “Denials increased 18% this month, primarily driven by eligibility-related rejections.”
* “Colorado Access claims are averaging 12 additional days in AR.”
* “Collections improved after resubmission workflow changes.”

THAT makes the system feel intelligent.

Not just decorative metrics.

* soften borders
* slightly increase border radius
* add subtle shadows
* use muted backgrounds
* reduce all caps labels
